The actual mandate

Build the connective tissue between innovation and operational trust.

GDIT's current VIA strategy emphasizes faster mission innovation, border security, AI, commercial partnerships and operational execution. This role translates that enterprise push into services CBP analysts can use, explain and improve.

What the mission protects

Analyst judgment, evidentiary integrity, security boundaries, mission continuity and accountability cannot be traded away for novelty.

AND

What the mission needs

Faster experimentation, scalable cloud services, real-time network intelligence and a shorter path from operational question to useful capability.

Without a shared service pathPrototypes stay isolated, integration is rediscovered and wave gates become administrative rather than operational.
Without visible evidenceAnalysts receive outputs faster but cannot reconstruct why the system reached them or when to override it.
Without ownership transferInnovation teams ship demonstrations while operational teams inherit risk, support debt and unclear decision rights.

First 90 days

Learn the mission, define the release path, prove one reusable pattern.

The first objective is not an architecture presentation. It is a shared view of analyst work, service boundaries and the evidence required to move capability safely.

Days 0-30

Read the mission system

  • Observe analyst questions, handoffs and escalation points.
  • Map enterprise services, data dependencies and current wave gates.
  • Clarify decision rights across NTC, GDIT, security and IC partners.
  • Inventory active prototypes and inherited technical debt.
Outputs: mission-service map, stakeholder topology, current-state release-path assessment.
Days 31-60

Install the airlock

  • Define reference service contracts and architecture patterns.
  • Set minimum lineage, explanation, security and ownership evidence.
  • Prioritize one analyst-facing capability with reusable value.
  • Build governance into the backlog and sprint definition of done.
Outputs: reference architecture, evidence standard, prioritized prototype backlog.
Days 61-90

Prove and transfer

  • Advance one capability through a representative wave gate.
  • Run analyst feedback, A/B learning and operational-readiness review.
  • Measure decision latency, adoption, lineage completeness and exception closure.
  • Brief leadership on scale, hold or stop decisions.
Outputs: capability scorecard, deployment recommendation, next-wave investment brief.

Open the detailed entry plan

“Where is the direct CBP OIT experience?”

I would not pretend that adjacent experience is the same as carrying a CBP badge or knowing every NTC operating nuance. The role deserves that honesty.

What transfers is the operating work: entering complex technical environments, learning from domain experts, making invisible dependencies visible, building governance into delivery and moving emerging technology from concept toward accountable use. My first 30 days are designed to earn mission context before prescribing architecture.

Executive discovery questions

The questions I would use to find the real constraint.

  1. Which analyst decision is currently most limited by data access, explanation or service latency?
  2. Where do promising prototypes most often stall between proof and enterprise operation?
  3. Which cloud migration wave gate creates the most rework because evidence arrives too late?
  4. What decision rights belong to the Enterprise Services lead versus architecture, security and mission product owners?
  5. How does the Cross-Domain Working Group distinguish a reusable enterprise pattern from a one-off exception?
  6. What would make an NTC analyst say, six months from now, “this changed how I work”?
